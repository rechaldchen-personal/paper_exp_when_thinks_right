# Abstract

**Title**: Commitment Dynamics in the J-Space: When Do Language Models Commit to Answers, and What Happens Under False-Lead Temptation?

---

Language models must commit to answers progressively through their network layers. But **when** do they commit, and **what happens when plausible wrong answers are initially tempting?** We study answer commitment in a mechanistic residual-stream readout—the Jacobian lens—which maps each layer's activations directly into predicted logits without running the forward pass through upper layers.

On 156 stimuli spanning factual knowledge, arithmetic reasoning, and garden-path sentences (72 strictly matched straightforward/false-lead pairs, 24 per family, plus 12 hard controls with no tempting distractor), we pre-registered three hypotheses about false-lead effects—when a prompt initially suggests an incorrect answer before revealing the correct one:

1. **H1 (Delayed Commitment)**: The layer where models lock in the target answer (ℓ*) occurs **later** under false-lead than straightforward conditions.

2. **H2 (Internal Oscillation)**: The top-1 predicted token **oscillates** (changes identity) in middle layers under false-lead, indicating real-time backtracking and revision.

3. **H3 (Dissociation Gap)**: A critical **confidence-correctness mismatch** emerges: models become confident (entropy collapses at layer ℓ_H) but are still wrong (target hasn't ranked top-1 yet). The gap ℓ* − ℓ_H measures how long models "confidently hallucinate" before correcting course.

For each hypothesis we report two pre-registered readouts: a **primary** metric over the full vocabulary (as originally specified) and a **confirmatory** metric restricted to {target, distractor}, added because the primary oscillation metric cannot distinguish wavering between the two candidate answers from churn among unrelated tokens—only the former would support a "revision" interpretation. The interpretation rule for every combination of outcomes was fixed before this run (`PRE_REGISTRATION_AMENDMENT.md`), and both readouts are reported regardless of which supports the hypothesis. We apply the Jacobian lens to Qwen3-1.7B and Qwen3-4B (each with its own freshly fitted lens on 1000×128 FineWeb prompts) and evaluate one-tailed exact signed-rank tests on a pre-registered 60/40 dev/holdout split, gated by a per-family behavioral pre-screen (all families, both models, cleared 70%+ straightforward top-1 accuracy). As a further robustness check, both models were also read out via logit-lens (identity transport), independent of the fitted Jacobian lens.

**Results on Qwen3-1.7B.** H3 is the best-supported finding: the dissociation gap is larger under false-lead in both the primary (median Δ=+1.0 layer, p=0.011, n=14 pairs) and confirmatory readout (median Δ=+4.0 layers, p=0.0016, n=18). The confirmatory version is remarkably robust — significant across every pre-registered θ (70th/80th/90th percentile) and band ([0.20,0.95] through [0.30,0.85]) setting, *and* under the independent logit-lens readout (p=0.034) — while the primary version holds only near the pre-registered default and under the primary jlens readout. **H2 does not support a revision interpretation** at any setting or readout tested: the primary oscillation metric is significant under jlens at the default θ (median Δ=+0.5, p=0.0077) but not under logit-lens (p=0.45) nor at θ=70/90; the confirmatory target-vs-distractor version is a stable null everywhere (p=0.27–0.46 across all four model/readout combinations reported below), and hard-control items with no tempting distractor oscillate about as much as false-lead items. **H1** is null under the primary metric but significant under the confirmatory one under both readouts (jlens p=0.0021, logit-lens p=0.040), robust across every θ/band setting though markedly weaker under logit-lens.

**Results on Qwen3-4B: none of H1–H3 replicate, under either readout.** All six tests (primary and confirmatory × H1/H2/H3) that were significant on 1.7B are null on 4B (p ranging 0.061–0.469; median effect sizes near zero, not merely noisy). Sample sizes are, if anything, larger at 4B, ruling out a simple power explanation. Behavioral and internal evidence points to why: Qwen3-4B is both more accurate (100% vs. 70.8% straightforward accuracy on arithmetic) and substantially less internally tempted by the false-lead framing than 1.7B — the false-lead distractor reaches the model's output top-5 only 4.2% of the time on arithmetic at 4B versus 29.2% at 1.7B, and the internal distractor-competition window (median `distractor_lead_layers`, false-lead minus straightforward) shrinks roughly threefold, from +4.5 layers at 1.7B to +1.5 at 4B.

This framework unifies mechanistic interpretability (where do answers emerge?) with cognitive science (how do minds revise initial commitments?), but the present data support a narrower and more conditional claim than that framing originally suggested. Within Qwen3-1.7B, the **confidence–correctness dissociation** (H3) is a genuinely robust signature — stable across every threshold, band, and lens-readout choice we tested — and delayed target-vs-distractor commitment (H1, confirmatory) holds in the same sense. But **neither effect generalizes to Qwen3-4B**, and the behavioral data suggest why: the effect may require a model susceptible enough to actually be internally tempted by the false-lead framing, which 4B largely is not. We report this as a substantive finding about scale- or capability-dependence, not as a failed replication to be minimized — a within-model-robust, cross-model-fragile effect is itself an important and underappreciated failure mode for single-model interpretability claims. Oscillation (H2) is real at the 1.7B/jlens/default-θ setting but its mechanism — candidate revision versus general instability — remains unsupported across every other setting and both models tested, and we report it as such rather than as a resolved "internal backtracking" signature.
